Fracture Healing

Fast study guide for a 15-minute pass. Focus on recognition, explanation, and the next useful action for this basic-science topic.

✓

Distinguish primary from secondary bone healing

This is the language that connects fixation stability to what kind of healing you expect to see.

4 min must know I can compare primary and secondary healing
Learning objective Explain when fracture healing occurs with direct remodeling versus callus formation.

Key concepts

  • Primary healing occurs with very low strain and stable compression; there is little visible callus.
  • Secondary healing occurs with relative stability; callus bridges the fracture through staged repair.
  • The fixation construct changes the biology you expect to see on follow-up films.

What to say out loud

  • Primary healing is direct bone remodeling across a stable fracture gap.
  • Secondary healing uses callus because controlled motion stimulates endochondral repair.

Common confusion

  • No callus after rigid compression is not automatically failure; it may reflect primary healing.

Sequence the stages of secondary healing

The stages explain why fracture appearance and mechanical strength change over time.

4 min must know I can sequence secondary healing
Learning objective Describe inflammation, soft callus, hard callus, and remodeling in order.

Key concepts

  • Inflammation recruits cells and creates the early repair environment.
  • Soft callus provides early bridging but is not mechanically mature.
  • Hard callus mineralizes and increases stability.
  • Remodeling reshapes bone along stress over months to years.

What to recognize

  • Early x-rays may lag behind symptoms and biology.
  • Bridging callus across cortices is more reassuring than callus in only one view.

Self-check question

  • Why might a fracture feel better before the x-ray looks fully healed?

Explain why excessive strain prevents bone formation

Mechanical environment is the bridge between fracture biology and fixation decisions.

4 min must know I can connect strain to union
Learning objective Connect stability, gap size, and interfragmentary strain to union or nonunion.

Key concepts

  • Bone formation tolerates less strain than fibrous tissue.
  • Large gaps and unstable constructs can keep strain too high for mineralized callus.
  • Relative stability is useful when motion is controlled, not uncontrolled.

Clinical application

  • Hypertrophic nonunion suggests biology is present but stability is inadequate.
  • Atrophic nonunion suggests biology, blood supply, or infection must be addressed.

Recognize delayed union, nonunion, and malunion

These failure states are the clinical reason fracture-healing biology matters.

3 min core I can recognize healing failure states
Learning objective Define the major healing failure states and the first question each should trigger.

What to recognize

  • Delayed union means healing is slower than expected but still progressing.
  • Nonunion means progression has stalled and needs a mechanical or biologic explanation.
  • Malunion means the fracture healed in an unacceptable position.

What to say out loud

  • For nonunion, ask whether the problem is stability, biology, infection, or host factors.

Self-check question

  • Which is more likely to need improved stability: hypertrophic or atrophic nonunion?

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

Fast study guide for a 15-minute pass. Focus on recognition, explanation, and the next useful action for this clinic topic.

✓

Recognize the median-nerve symptom pattern

CTS is usually a clinical diagnosis, so the history has to come first.

4 min must know I can recognize CTS symptoms
Learning objective Identify classic carpal tunnel symptoms and severity clues.

What to recognize

  • Numbness and paresthesias in the thumb, index, middle, and radial ring finger.
  • Night symptoms or shaking the hand out are classic.
  • Thenar weakness or atrophy suggests more advanced compression.

Common confusion

  • Whole-hand numbness, neck pain, or ulnar-sided symptoms should make you consider mimics.

Perform a focused CTS exam

The exam should confirm distribution, severity, and alternate diagnoses.

4 min must know I can examine CTS
Learning objective Describe the exam maneuvers and motor findings that matter for CTS.

Key concepts

  • Check median sensory distribution and compare sides.
  • Assess thenar bulk and thumb abduction strength.
  • Use provocative maneuvers as supporting data, not the whole diagnosis.

What to say out loud

  • I would document sensation, thenar strength/atrophy, and provocative testing, then screen for cervical or ulnar mimics.

Know when electrodiagnostics help

Testing is most useful when the diagnosis is unclear, severity matters, or surgery is being considered.

2 min core I can use electrodiagnostics appropriately
Learning objective Explain when EMG/NCS is helpful in CTS workup.

Key concepts

  • Many classic cases are diagnosed clinically.
  • Electrodiagnostics can confirm diagnosis, grade severity, and evaluate mimics.
  • Severe findings or denervation support urgency of decompression.

Self-check question

  • When would you order EMG before treating CTS?

Explain indications for carpal tunnel release

Treatment should follow severity, chronicity, and failure of nonoperative care.

4 min must know I can explain release indications
Learning objective Distinguish splinting/injection candidates from patients who need release.

Key concepts

  • Night splinting is common first-line treatment for mild symptoms.
  • Injection can be diagnostic and therapeutic in selected patients.
  • Thenar weakness, atrophy, severe studies, or persistent symptoms despite nonoperative care support release.

What to say out loud

  • Surgery is to decompress the median nerve by releasing the transverse carpal ligament.

Identify structures released during carpal tunnel surgery

You do not need every technical detail, but you should know the target and what must be protected.

1 min core I can describe release anatomy
Learning objective Describe the goal of carpal tunnel release and the key structure at risk.

Clinical or operative application

  • The transverse carpal ligament is released to decompress the median nerve.
  • The recurrent motor branch is a key danger structure.
  • Persistent symptoms, pillar pain, and nerve injury are complications to remember.

Common confusion

  • The operation is not a tendon release; the goal is median nerve decompression.

Posterior Hip Approach

Fast study guide for a 15-minute pass. Focus on recognition, explanation, and the next useful action for this approach topic.

✓

Set up lateral positioning and posterior landmarks

Positioning and landmarks determine whether the rest of the exposure makes sense.

3 min must know I can orient the approach
Learning objective Describe lateral positioning and the surface landmarks for the posterior hip approach.

Key concepts

  • Patient is commonly positioned lateral decubitus.
  • Greater trochanter is the key bony landmark.
  • Incision and deep work orient around the posterior aspect of the greater trochanter.

Clinical or operative application

  • Watch how the pelvis is stabilized before prepping and draping.

Follow superficial and deep exposure

The approach becomes less mysterious when each layer is tied to its purpose.

3 min must know I can follow the exposure
Learning objective Walk through gluteus maximus split, short external rotator release, and capsulotomy.

Clinical or operative application

  • Split gluteus maximus in line with fibers.
  • Identify, release, and tag short external rotators.
  • Open the posterior capsule to access the hip joint.

What to say out loud

  • The tagged rotators and capsule are important because they are repaired later for stability.

Identify short external rotators and capsule

These structures are not trivia; they are the soft-tissue repair that helps posterior stability.

3 min must know I can name the released structures
Learning objective Name the posterior soft tissues released and repaired during the approach.

Key concepts

  • Piriformis and the short external rotators are posterior landmarks.
  • The posterior capsule is opened for access and commonly repaired.
  • Tagging sutures help find and repair these structures at closure.

Common confusion

  • The posterior approach does not mean cutting the abductors as the main exposure.

Protect the sciatic nerve danger zone

The sciatic nerve is the must-not-miss danger structure for a posterior hip approach.

3 min must know I can identify the danger zone
Learning objective Explain where the sciatic nerve is at risk and how the team avoids injuring it.

What to recognize

  • The sciatic nerve lies posterior and medial relative to the short external rotator region.
  • Blind medial dissection and aggressive retraction increase risk.

What to say out loud

  • I am watching the relationship between the short external rotators and sciatic nerve before deep medial work.

Connect closure to posterior stability

Closure is part of the operation's stability strategy, not just the end of the case.

3 min core I can explain posterior repair
Learning objective Explain why posterior capsule and rotator repair matter after posterior approach THA.

Clinical or operative application

  • Posterior capsule and short external rotators are repaired when possible.
  • Soft-tissue tension, component position, and precautions all affect stability.
  • Posterior dislocation risk is the complication to connect back to closure.

Self-check question

  • Why does posterior repair matter after the implants are already in?
